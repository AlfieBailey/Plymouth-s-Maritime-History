Key events in Plymouth's Maritime History

As previously stated, Plymouth's maritime history is one that is very rich, in large part due to it being by the coastline and facing in the direction of regions such as mainland Europe. In this timeline shown below, you will be able to see all of the biggest events in Plymouth's maritime history in chronological order. Many explorations have been completed from Plymouth, as well as it being a key place in terms of stopping raids. This is no new concept, either, as this has been the case, at least as we know it, for coming up to a millennium now.

1211

This was the first recorded time that cargo had left Plymouth

1528

During the next three centuries Plymouth established its reputation both as a centre for voyage and discovery, and for its military importance. Transatlantic trade originated with William Hawkins in 1528

1572

Francis Drake was the first Englishman to sail in the Pacific

1577

Francis Drake then started a circumnavigation of the globe, a feat only 1 person had done before, and no Englishman had ever done

1580

It was mission complete for Drake, in regards to his attempts to circumnavigate the globe. His mission was accomplished

1588

Back in Plymouth, Francis Drake then masterminded the defeat of the Spanish Armada, who sailed from Lisbon to Plymouth, only to be defeated

1620

Perhaps the most celebrated expedition to leave Plymouth was that of the Pilgrim Fathers. Persecuted for their puritan beliefs in eastern England, they set sail for the New World on board the Mayflower in 1620

1670

Plymouth’s military expansion began in earnest in 1670 when a citadel was built on the highest point above the town, the Hoe, meaning high ground

1690

This was when the first Royal Dockyard opened on the banks of the Tamar west of Plymouth. Further docks were built in 1727, 1762 and 1793, and a huge naval complex was later established, including the communities of Plymouth Dock and Stonehouse

1768

Further explorations that left from Plymouth included three voyages to the Southern Ocean and the Pacific made by James Cook, the first of which being in 1768

1934

At the Barbican, too, is the Mayflower Steps monument, built in 1934 and a permanent reminder of the Pilgrims’ voyage to America

1967

In 1967 Sir Francis Chichester completed the first ever solo circumnavigation of the globe on board his yacht Gypsy Moth IV. He started and ended this expedition at Plymouth
